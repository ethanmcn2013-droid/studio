# Motion-Graphics Freelancer — Equity-for-Work Memo
## A different animal from the Founder Circle gift
## v1.0 — 2026-06-01 · STATUS: OPTION UNDER CONSIDERATION (not yet decided)

> **Status: DRAFT decision memo.** Ethan is *considering* offering ~2.5% to a motion-graphics freelancer in exchange for completing work, "so he has real value in the company succeeding." Not decided. Tax figures carry a Jan 2026 cutoff — verify. Not regulated advice.

---

## 1. The one thing that must not happen

**Do not put the freelancer inside the Founder Circle gift framework.** The Founder Circle is built on *gift for natural love and affection* — that framing is what makes Mam's transfer near-tax-free and emotionally coherent. A freelancer receiving shares **in exchange for services** is the legal and tax opposite, and mixing them contaminates both:
- it muddies the gift basis of the family transfers (Revenue could question whether *those* were really gifts);
- it cheapens the Founder Circle brand ("you mattered before this existed" does not describe a paid vendor);
- it gives the wrong person the wrong rights (the agreement's clauses are written for a loved one, not a commercial counterparty).

If equity goes to the freelancer, it goes via a **separate Services-for-Equity Agreement**, as a **separate commercial holder** — not as a Founder Circle member, not on the SS-FC certificate series.

---

## 2. The tax reality (his, and yours)

Shares given as payment for work are **taxable income to the freelancer**, not a CAT gift:
- The **market value of the shares at the time he receives them** is treated as **income** — income tax + USC + PRSI, or, if he's a self-employed contractor, **taxable trading income** (a payment-in-kind for his invoice).
- At a near-nominal valuation (same logic as the tax memo), that value is *small*, so the absolute tax is small — but the **obligation exists** and he must account for it. He should invoice for the work and recognise the share value as consideration received.
- For the **company**, issuing shares for services rendered is a cost (the motion work) — recognised/expensed accordingly.

There is no family threshold to shelter this. It is remuneration.

---

## 3. The dilution interaction with Mam (important)

Mam's agreement has **full anti-dilution to 10%** (clause 8). If you later issue 2.5% to the freelancer:
- Mam is **topped back up to 10% at nil cost**, and
- **all** of that dilution falls on **Ethan's Class A** — not split proportionally.

Worked sketch (illustrative): issue freelancer shares = 2.5% of the enlarged company, restore Mam to 10% → Ethan absorbs the freelancer's 2.5% **and** Mam's top-up. Ethan drops from 90% to roughly **87%**, Mam holds 10%, freelancer ~2.5%. That is fine and intentional (founder absorbs dilution by design) — but **know it before you offer**, because the "2.5%" you give away costs *you* more than 2.5% once Mam's protection re-rates.

---

## 4. Is equity even the right tool here?

Push-back worth hearing before you offer:

- **Illiquidity.** This is a founder-led company with no outside investors and no exit planned. The freelancer's only path to cash from equity is the SSV buy-back — at the company's discretion, when it can afford it. That is a weak "real value in the company succeeding" for someone who mostly wants to be paid for work.
- **Alignment without equity.** If the goal is shared upside, consider lighter instruments: a **milestone bonus**, a **revenue-share on work he contributes to**, or a **cash fee now + a small discretionary equity grant later if the relationship deepens**. These align him without permanently widening the cap table or creating a tax event on day one.
- **Permanence.** Equity is forever (subject to buy-back); a freelancer relationship usually isn't. Giving 2.5% for a bounded body of work is a high price for an illiquid minority stake that he may not value at its illiquid worth.

**My recommendation:** unless this freelancer is becoming a long-term creative partner (not just delivering the 8 launch videos), **pay cash and offer a milestone/revenue-share for upside** rather than equity. If you do want him to hold equity, do it deliberately as a **separate Class (or clearly-separate Class B grant) under a Services-for-Equity Agreement with vesting tied to deliverables**, priced and taxed as income, kept entirely outside the Founder Circle. Dissent noted in case you disagree: a small, motivating equity stake *can* be the cheapest way to secure a scarce creative collaborator's commitment for the long haul — if that's genuinely what he is.
