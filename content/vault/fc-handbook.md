## Page 1 — Why Signal Studio Exists

Most tools for managing projects were built for software teams. They assume you have engineers, sprints, tickets. They assume you work in an organisation large enough to have a dedicated product team.

Most people do not.

Signal Studio was built for the majority — the event planner managing fifteen moving parts with no dedicated software, the venue coordinator tracking every supplier in a spreadsheet, the small business owner running a project from a notes app because nothing else fits how they actually work.

The tools that exist were not designed for them. They were designed for a different kind of worker and then handed to everyone else and expected to fit. They mostly do not.

Signal Studio is an attempt to build something that does.

---

## Page 2 — The Mission

*"Project management for the 80% not in tech."*

That is the clearest way to say it.

The 80% are not unsophisticated. They are experienced, capable people managing complexity that would challenge any team. What they lack is not skill — it is tools that were designed with them in mind from the beginning, not adapted for them afterward.

Signal Studio is building those tools. Not a single product, but a suite: a task manager, a roadmap tool, an analytics layer, and a capture surface for notes. Each is a standalone product, useful on its own. Together, they form something more complete.

The aim is not the largest possible user base. The aim is the right tools for the people who need them — built well and sustained by people who find them genuinely useful.

---

## Page 3 — The Long-Term Vision

Signal Studio is four products. The vision is not more products — it is those four products, made excellent.

There is a belief inside Signal Studio that the best software does not try to do everything. It does a small number of things, and it does them in a way that makes you trust it. That trust — earned slowly, through good decisions and sustained quality — is what the business is built on.

The long-term vision is a suite of products that people keep using not because they are locked in, but because they are well made, fairly priced, and honest about what they do.

Honest software is not easy to build. It requires saying no more often than yes. It requires building slowly when the market wants you to move fast. It requires treating quality as a non-negotiable rather than a feature you add later.

That is not a fast vision. Most worthwhile things are not.

## Page 5 — What Ownership Means

You hold Class B Founder Circle Shares in Signal Studio.

**What these shares include:**

*Dividend participation.* When Signal Studio distributes profit, you receive a proportional share. The proportion corresponds to your share percentage — ten percent for the Founding Member, two and a half percent for each Founder Circle Member.

*Enterprise value participation.* If Signal Studio is ever acquired or sold, you participate in the proceeds in proportion to your shares.

*Quarterly updates.* A brief founder note, four times a year — a real account of how the business is going. Not investor relations. A note.

*Annual reflection.* Once a year, a more considered account: what happened, what changed, what the coming year looks like.

**What these shares do not include:**

*Management authority.* Operational decisions are made by the founder. That is the structure and it is intentional.

*Board authority.* There is no board at this stage. If one is formed in the future, Founder Circle shares do not carry board representation.

*Veto rights.* You cannot block a decision.

The framing that matters: you share in the upside without carrying the weight. The decisions, the risk, the daily work — that is Ethan's. The benefit, if Signal Studio builds value, is shared with you.

## Page 6 — How Dividends Work

Dividends are not guaranteed. They depend on profit, and profit is not guaranteed — particularly in the early years of a business being built without outside investment.

When Signal Studio is profitable and chooses to distribute, the calculation is as follows:

```
Revenue
  Less:   operating expenses
  Less:   tax
  Less:   operating reserve
  Less:   growth investment
  ═══════════════════════════
  =       distributable profit
```

Distributable profit is then divided proportionally across all shares.

At 10%, the Founding Member receives ten percent of distributable profit.
At 2.5% each, the four Founder Circle Members each receive two and a half percent.
The remaining 80% returns to the founder.

No timeline is promised. No minimum is promised. What is promised: if there is profit to distribute and the decision is made to distribute it, you will receive your proportional share, without delay and without complication.

**A note on the growth investment deduction:** This exists to allow the business to reinvest when reinvestment is the right call. A business that distributes everything before it is ready to is worth less, not more. The deduction is there to protect the long-term value of your shares, not to reduce them.

## Page 7 — Founder-Led Governance

Signal Studio is founder-led. That means Ethan makes the decisions.

This is not a statement about trust. The people in this circle are trusted precisely because they are in it.

It is a statement about how good work gets done at this stage of building a business. Fast, clear decisions. No approval processes. No review committees. The ability to change direction when the evidence calls for it, to experiment without a consensus, to commit without waiting.

Founder-led businesses succeed or fail at this stage largely based on the quality of one person's judgment. Distributing that decision-making does not usually improve the judgment. It slows it and diffuses it.

As Signal Studio grows and the right moment arrives, governance will evolve. That decision belongs to the future, made with more information about what the business actually requires. For now: one person makes the calls. The others share in what those calls produce.

There is a particular kind of integrity required of a founder-led structure: the founder cannot hide behind the structure. Every decision is theirs. Every outcome, good or bad, is theirs. The Founder Circle is not responsible for those outcomes — but the founder is accountable to them for the quality of the judgment being exercised on their behalf.

## Page 8 — Quarterly Founder Updates

Four times a year, you will receive a note from Ethan.

Not a financial report. Not a polished quarterly briefing. A note — the kind written to someone who is owed a real account of how things are going.

It will cover what the business has been working on. What is going well and what is not. What changed since the last note. What the coming quarter looks like. If there is a difficult thing to report, it will be reported.

The format will stay roughly consistent so it is easy to read. The honesty will not vary.

Once a year — either as the final quarter note or as a separate document — there will be a more considered reflection: what the year produced, what was learned, and what the year ahead requires.

These updates are not performance management. They are not designed to project confidence or manage perceptions. They exist because you are part of something and you should know, genuinely, how that thing is going.

If you have a question after reading an update, you can ask. Ethan will answer.

## Page 9 — Long-Term Philosophy

Signal Studio is built with a particular belief about what good software requires: time, patience, and a refusal to optimise for the wrong things.

The products are built to be excellent before they are marketed. The business is built to be sustainable before it scales. The relationships — with users, with the people in this circle — are built to last.

There is a version of a software business that grows quickly by cutting corners — on product quality, on user experience, on the honest acknowledgement of what the software can and cannot do. That version can be commercially successful in the short term. It tends not to be the kind of thing worth building.

Signal Studio is not trying to grow as fast as possible. It is trying to build something that is still worth using in ten years — and still worth running in ten years, for a founder who has not burned out trying to make it something it was never meant to be.

The measure that matters: do the products make people's work genuinely easier? Everything else — the revenue, the growth, the recognition — follows from that, or it does not come at all.
