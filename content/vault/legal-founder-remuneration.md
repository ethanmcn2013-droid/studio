# Founder Remuneration — How Ethan Takes Money Out
## "Drawings" in a limited company · v1.0 — 2026-06-02

> **Status: DRAFT framework for DIY.** Irish tax rates/thresholds carry a Jan-2026 cutoff — **verify every rate at revenue.ie before acting.** Not regulated tax/accounting advice. This sits alongside the incorporation package; it concerns the *Founder's* (Class A) income, distinct from Mam's Class B gift.

---

## 1. The conceptual correction

In a **sole trade or partnership**, "drawings" are real: the business is you, and you withdraw freely (taxed on profits regardless).

In a **limited company**, there are **no drawings.** Signal Studio Limited is a separate legal person. Its money is the *company's*, not yours. If you take money out without characterising it, it is automatically a **director's loan** (you owe the company back), which carries its own tax cost (§4c). So the real question is not "how do I take drawings" but **"through which route do I extract money, and what's the tax on each."**

There are five routes. Use the cheap ones; avoid the expensive ones.

---

## 2. The five routes (at a glance)

| Route | Taxed how | Reduces company's CT? | Triggers Mam's 10%? |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **a. Salary (PAYE)** | Income tax + USC + PRSI on you | **Yes** (deductible) | **No** |
| **b. Dividend** | Income tax + USC + PRSI on you, *after* 12.5% CT already paid (double tax) | No | **YES — pro rata** |
| **c. Director's loan (company → you)** | Triggers a company tax charge until repaid + BIK | No | No |
| **d. Director's loan repaid (you → company, drawn back)** | **Tax-free** (it's your own money back) | No | No |
| **e. Employer pension contribution** | Tax-free now; taxed in retirement | **Yes** (deductible) | No |
| **f. Vouched expenses** | **Tax-free** reimbursement | Yes (expense) | No |

---

## 3. The route that matters most early — your director's loan account (3d)

You have been **funding Signal Studio personally** (infrastructure, the motion videos, legal, the €15,099 cost base in the plan). Every euro you have put in, the company **owes you back.**

- Keep a **director's loan account** — a running record of money you've lent the company (paid for its costs personally, or transferred in) vs. money you've drawn back.
- As the company earns cash, you can **withdraw to repay that loan — entirely tax-free**, because it's the return of your own after-tax money. No income tax, no PRSI, no USC.
- **This is your main legitimate "drawing" in Year 1**, when your salary is deliberately €0 (per the business plan). Track it from day one — receipts, transfers, a simple ledger. It is the cleanest money you can take out.

**Action:** start a loan-account ledger now (even pre-incorporation costs can be reimbursed once the company exists, if properly vouched and for the company's benefit — verify the pre-incorporation expense rules).

---

## 4. The other routes, in order of usefulness

**a. Salary / PAYE — your route for ongoing income.**
When you want a real wage (the plan defers this past Year 1), pay yourself a salary. It is **deductible**, so it reduces the company's 12.5% corporation tax, and it is taxed once in your hands. To do this: register the company as an **employer** with Revenue, operate **payroll** (real-time PAYE reporting via ROS). A salary does **not** trigger any payment to Mam — it's remuneration for work, not a shareholder distribution. The SSV valuation already assumes a market-rate founder cost, so paying yourself is "priced in," not value-destroying.

**e. Employer pension — the efficient long lever.**
The company can make **employer pension contributions** for you: corporation-tax deductible, no benefit-in-kind, and not subject to the salary tax stack. For an owner-manager this is one of the most efficient ways to move money from company to you, for later. Bring it in once there's spare cash. *(Verify current contribution limits.)*

**f. Expenses — small but free.**
Genuine business expenses you pay personally are **reimbursed tax-free** (vouched). Civil-service **mileage and subsistence** rates can be paid for business travel. Plus the **small benefit exemption** (non-cash, up to the annual limit — verify, ~€1,000) for a couple of vouchers a year.

**b. Dividend — the expensive route, and it's an "everyone" lever.**
Two reasons to avoid dividends for your *personal* income:
1. **Double taxation.** A dividend is paid from profits *already* taxed at 12.5% CT, then taxed **again** as income in your hands (income tax up to 40% + USC + PRSI). Ireland has **no preferential dividend rate** (unlike the UK). Dividend Withholding Tax of 25% (verify) is deducted and credited.
2. **It triggers Mam's 10%.** The Class A and Class B shares rank **pari passu for dividends** (constitution Clause C, agreement clause 3/5). So you **cannot pay yourself a dividend without paying Mam her 10% pro rata.** That is by design — dividends are the deliberate *Founder Circle distribution* mechanism, governed by the dividend framework and the SSV. **Do not use dividends as your personal salary substitute.** Reserve them for the intentional, scheduled Founder Circle distributions.

**c. Director's loan (company lends to you) — avoid except short-term.**
If the company lends *you* money (i.e. you overdraw), Signal Studio is a **close company** (controlled by ≤5 participators), so a loan to a participator triggers a company income-tax charge under **s.438 TCA** — broadly the loan grossed up at the standard rate, paid over to Revenue and **refunded when you repay**. An interest-free loan also creates a **benefit-in-kind** on you. Net: messy and costly. Fine for a brief timing overdraw you clear quickly; not a strategy.

---

## 5. The clean design (why the structure works)

The Founder Circle gift and your remuneration **don't collide**, because the two non-dividend routes you'll actually use don't touch Mam:

- **Salary, pension, expense reimbursement, and loan-account repayment** → flow to **you only**, no Class B trigger.
- **Dividends** → the *one* shared lever, used deliberately for Founder Circle distributions per the dividend framework, where Mam *should* get her 10%.

So: **take your income via salary / loan-repayment / pension; let dividends be the Founder Circle event.** Clean separation, and Mam shares in the upside exactly where she's meant to (real profit distribution and a sale), not in your wages.

---

## 6. One trap to verify — the close-company surcharge

Irish **close companies** can face a **surcharge** on certain **undistributed** income:
- ~20% on undistributed investment/rental income (**s.440 TCA**);
- a surcharge on undistributed professional income of a **"service company"** (**s.441 TCA**).

Whether a software-product company is a "service company" in the s.441 sense is **not obvious and worth checking** — if it were caught, retaining profits rather than paying them out (as salary/pension/dividend) within ~18 months could attract a surcharge. This is one of the few places where the answer genuinely affects strategy (distribute vs retain). **Verify the service-company definition and surcharge exposure** — and note that paying yourself a salary/pension reduces retained profit and therefore exposure.

---

## 7. Recommended sequence for you

1. **Now / Year 1 (salary €0):** track the **director's loan account**; draw back your own contributions tax-free as cash allows; reimburse **vouched expenses**.
2. **When you want ongoing income:** register as employer, start a **PAYE salary** at a sensible level (deductible).
3. **When there's spare cash:** add **employer pension** contributions (efficient).
4. **Dividends:** only as the deliberate **Founder Circle distribution**, per the dividend framework — never as your personal wage.
5. **Check** the close-company surcharge exposure so you're not penalised for retaining profit.
