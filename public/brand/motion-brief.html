Signal Studio · Motion & Advertising Reference Brief

The Film System.

One indigo dot. Five gestures. Thirty films. This is the source of truth for every motion-graphic and advertising piece Signal Studio commissions — the strategy behind the work, the motion language every film inherits, and a phased plan for what to build first.

The dot is the hero.

How to use this brief

This document does three jobs. It explains why Signal Studio films look and move the way they do (§1). It hands you the system every film is built from — gestures, palette, type, sound, and the words allowed on screen (§2). And it gives you a plan: thirty films, categorised and sequenced by cost, reuse, and dependency, so the cheapest, highest-leverage work happens first (§3–§4).

If you're a motion designer

Start at §2 (the motion language) and your film's detail card. Everything you need — duration, technique, sound, exact on-screen copy — is in the card. Do not deviate from the copy.

If you're a director or partner

Read §1 and §4. The strategy tells you what each film is for; the plan tells you which to greenlight and in what order. Pitch ideas against the refusal checklist (§Reference).

If you're an AI tool

Treat §2's copy rules and the refusal checklist as hard constraints. Any output containing a banned word or an exclamation mark is rejected before review. Brand voice wins over any persuasion formula.

The one rule above all rules

Signal Studio films are calm, declarative, and quiet. No exclamation marks. No hype words. The work earns its quality through craft and restraint — it never claims it. If a frame feels like it's shouting, it's wrong.

Section 1

Strategy

What every film is trying to do, and the through-line that makes thirty separate pieces read as one brand.

The hero is a dot

Signal Studio's entire identity is a single indigo dot. It is the favicon, the wordmark's period, the loading state, and — across this whole reel — the main character. The dot is not a logo we place in a corner; it is the subject of the films. This is the discipline that lets us make thirty films cheaply and coherently: we are animating one object, with one colour, on paper.

The dot reads time. It behaves differently in each product, and those five behaviours (§2) are our motion alphabet. A viewer who watches three of our films should be able to recognise a fourth as ours from the motion alone — before any logo appears.

Belief, not just comprehension

Films do one of two jobs. Comprehension films show what a product does. Belief films make someone feel this brand is for them and has a spine. A reel of only comprehension films explains a tool nobody has a reason to care about; a reel of only belief films wins hearts for a product nobody understands. This brief is balanced on purpose, and sequenced so comprehension (cheap, 2D) comes first and belief (expensive, live) comes once the motion language is proven.

Who we're talking to — the 80%

Signal Studio is project management for the 80% not in tech: the wedding planner, the tradesperson quoting from a van, the tutor between two students, the shop owner, the freelancer with eleven live clients. They are the person the work routes through, in a category that doesn't call itself an industry. Every film casts their work and their words — never "engineering teams", never jargon, never stock-photo office life.

The moat is refusal

Our competitive edge isn't a feature — anyone can copy a feature. It's disciplined refusal, sustained across a suite. We refuse jargon (sprint, burndown, velocity, stakeholder), refuse AI-marketing, refuse the dashboard, refuse the configuration tax. Several films in this reel are about what we won't build. Refusal is not a gap in the story; it is the story.

A note on the brief that started this

This work was commissioned with the words "Apple-grade" and "world-class". The films should absolutely be that — but the brand bans world-class, best-in-class, seamless, and supercharge as on-screen words. So the quality lives in the craft, the sound, and the restraint, and is demonstrated, never stated. The most distinctive film we have is literally about what we refuse to build. That paradox — show it, never say it — is the central creative principle of the entire system.

Reference register

Aspire to

Apple product films, Linear's motion spec, Arc, Notion Calendar, Raycast. Macro detail, single hero object, deliberate slow moves, tactile sound, negative space, one idea per shot.

Never

Jira / Monday / Tableau dashboard sizzle, holographic gradients, glow blooms, robot/AI motifs, fast-cut hustle edits, the "feature × icon × verb" 3-up SaaS grid.

Type

Geist Sans for everything; Geist Mono for eyebrows, timers, status labels, version stamps. Display headings: weight 600, letter-spacing −0.045em, line-height 0.96. Eyebrows: 11px mono, uppercase, tracked 0.14em. No Inter-alone, no Roboto, no Space Grotesk, never more than two families on screen.

Sound vocabulary

One audio system across the reel, tied to the gestures. Tactile, not musical. Silence is a legitimate choice — often the strongest one.

Pulse

Soft heartbeat sub-bass. Warmth, presence.

Sweep

Airy glide, held under lateral moves.

Tick

Dry mechanical step-click. Discrete, precise.

Caret

Clean blink tick; dampened keystrokes; warm commit tone.

Broadcast

A single deep bloom. Used once.

Negative space

True silence after noise. The punchline in several films.

Words allowed on screen

Copy is locked. Use the lines verbatim from each film's card and the library in the Reference section. The non-negotiables:

  • No exclamation marks. Anywhere. Ever.
  • Locked taglines: "Clarity, not configuration." · "Everything important. Nothing distracting." · "Project management for the 80% not in tech."
  • Locked CTAs: Open the workspace · Open the roadmap · Open the briefing · Open the notebook. Never "Get started", "Try it", "Sign up free".
  • Wordmark: signal studio. (lowercase, period) and tasks· timeline· signal· notes.. Never "Signal" alone.

See the full banned-word list and the refusal checklist in the Reference section.

Section 4

The production plan

Build cheap-and-reusable first; build expensive-and-emotional once the motion language is proven. Six phases, sequenced by cost, leverage, and dependency.

Phase 0 · Foundation~1–2 weeks · no public film

Lock the motion language

Before any film, produce a 15–20 second system test: the five gestures animated to their locked timings, the sound vocabulary recorded, the type and palette in motion. This is not advertising — it is the spec every later film inherits, and the thing you approve once so you never re-litigate it mid-production.

Why first: every other film is faster and more coherent once this exists. Output: a reference reel + a Lottie/AE source pack handed to every designer.

Deliverable: gesture + sound + type system test
Phase 1 · The 2D coreLowest cost · highest reuse

Product loops that double as the live site

The suite line plus one loop per product, all pure 2D. These are the films and the website hero animations (exportable as Lottie), so the spend funds marketing and product polish at once. They also establish the motion language in public before any money goes near a camera.

Take a position, in kinetic type

Brand films that argue the thesis without a single actor — refusal, restraint, plain English, the design system itself. These move the brand from "a tool" to "a point of view" while staying inside the AE/Lottie budget.

The flagship suite story and the emotional product films. Most are 2D-and-screen-capture hybrids; the demo UI must be real and honestly populated — no faked, over-full demo workspace. Produce these once the language is set, so they inherit a proven look.

The refusal checklist

Run every script, board, and cut against this before review. A single hit is a rejection, not a note.

Banned words on screen

  • AI · AI-powered · intelligent · smart · copilot · agent · assistant · autonomous
  • world-class · best-in-class · seamless · supercharge · transform · revolutionise · robust · turnkey
  • sprint · epic · burndown · velocity · stakeholder · kanban · backlog · OKR
  • Any exclamation mark, anywhere
  • 3-adjective trios ("Modern, simple, beautiful")

Banned visuals

  • Holographic gradients, glow blooms, robot / AI motifs
  • More than one accent colour; category-coloured products
  • Purple (#7c5cff) — purged suite-wide
  • Stock people, mascots, illustrated peoplework
  • Faked / over-populated demo workspaces (the demo-vs-reality gap)
  • The "feature × icon × verb" 3-up SaaS grid
  • Fast-cut hustle edits; confetti; gamification
Register-break governance

At most two films in the whole system may bend the calm register (currently #17, plus the benched "Project-Manager Energy"). A break never opens a reel. A break never uses an exclamation mark. Breaks mock a behaviour, never a person. Dignity films are cast with real operators — pity is a rejection.
