## Sales narrative

The following is a founder-to-venue-owner story. It is not a brochure. It is the shape of an honest conversation.

---

Most venues know the moment. A couple books. Everyone is warm. Then the emails start — questions already answered in the welcome pack, spreadsheet versions from three months ago, planners who have not seen the update you sent last week. Nothing about it is the couple's fault. They are managing one of the most complex personal projects most people ever take on, without any real tools for it, using WhatsApp threads and a shared Google Doc that someone stopped updating. The confusion is structural. It is not a personality problem.

That confusion reaches your team as admin. Not the interesting kind of admin — the repetitive kind. The same questions, the same reassurance, the same chasing of information that should have been in one place from the start. Over a full wedding season that adds up. It erodes the looked-after feeling your venue sells. Not dramatically — quietly. But a coordinator fielding the same question for the eighth time that month is not fully present for the thing they are actually there to do.

Signal Studio gives each couple a single calm place to plan. Notes to capture what they are thinking. Tasks to track what needs to happen. A Timeline they can share with suppliers and family so everyone is working from the same picture. And a morning briefing — three things, in plain English — so nothing slips. Four products. One calm place.

The Venue Edition puts your venue's name on it. Not as a logo wall — as a quiet line at the top of the workspace. An eyebrow. The couple's plan stays the thing in focus. Your name is just there, part of the experience, carrying the signal that this venue looks after you.

You pay €1,500 once a year. Every couple you are booking gets eighteen months of the full suite, then it drops to the free plan with a quiet prompt. No seats, no per-couple arithmetic, no software to run. Your team's only involvement is handing a code to a couple when they book. That is the whole operation on your side.

This is patronage, not software. You are not licensing a tool. You are standing behind your couples' planning. The value to you is not a feature list — it is fewer confused couples, fewer repetitive emails, and a planning experience that carries your venue's name and reflects its standard of care.

One sentence a generic competitor would never write: you do not pay to give couples more software. You pay to give them less noise.

## Pricing

The venue pays Signal Studio once a year, prepaid. The couple never sees a price.

### Size → price logic

| Venue size | Annual venues (approx.) | Annual fee |
|---|---|---|
| Every venue | Any size | €1,500 |

The logic: Signal Studio gives every couple the venue sends eighteen months of the full suite. One fixed annual price removes negotiation by venue size, keeps the offer legible, and leaves the value to compound as more couples use it. There is one Venue Edition, not a hidden set of enterprise tiers.

There is no per-couple maths and no seat counting. The venue pays the flat annual fee. Every couple booked in that year gets a code.

### Founding terms

The first fifteen venues lock €1,500 a year for as long as they stay. This is not an introductory rate. There is no clause that expires it into a higher number. The asset is permanence, not a temporary markdown. A founding venue that stays at €1,500 in year five has not missed a price increase — that is the deal, and it holds.

Why the founding lock still matters: the first fifteen venues are taking a position before there is a case study or a referral. Every venue pays €1,500 today; founding venues receive the contractual certainty that the number will not move for as long as they stay.

### What the venue gets

- Codes to pass to couples at booking (or at any point in the planning year).
- A co-branded workspace eyebrow — the venue name, quiet, at the top.
- One coordinator walkthrough (30 minutes, once — not a standing arrangement).
- A soft two-week redemption window for the first cohort, watched quietly.
- One short review at the end of the window. That is the whole commitment.
- Founding venues: first look at new work and a standing to shape what changes.

## Pilot offer

The founding-venue pilot is not a free trial. It is a paid founding position with a defined arc and a limited cohort.

### The arc

1. **Settle the year.** The venue pays €1,500, prepaid. One price applies to every venue.
2. **Codes and a short note.** Signal Studio sends the redemption codes and a brief note the venue can pass to couples at booking. The note does not require editing — it is written for the venue's voice to carry it.
3. **One walkthrough.** A coordinator gets a 30-minute walkthrough — what the workspace looks like for a couple, how the eyebrow appears, what happens at month 18. Once. Not a recurring training.
4. **Soft redemption window.** About two weeks. Couples redeem and start planning. Signal Studio watches quietly and stays out of the way. There is no live dashboard for the venue to monitor — the product runs itself.
5. **One review.** A short conversation. What worked. What did not. What couples asked. What the venue would change. That is the whole commitment — no recurring meetings, no reporting deck.

### What the pilot is not

It is not a free trial with an upgrade prompt. It is not a pilot that converts to a more expensive tier. €1,500 is the Venue Edition price. The review is the mechanism for shaping what comes next — not the mechanism for reselling.

## Objections

These are the real objections a premium venue owner raises. Each one is answered honestly.

**"Our couples already use a wedding planner."**

Most do. A planner does not replace this — they use it too. The planner stops chasing version control across WhatsApp and email because the couple's decisions live in one place. If the planner is good, they will want this. If the planner is resistant to anything digital, that is worth knowing — but it is not a reason for the venue to withhold something calmer for the couple. A venue handing out codes is not mandating a tool. It is offering a better option.

**"Our couples already use a spreadsheet."**

Some do. A spreadsheet is a document with rows. It does not capture a decision, track a task, or produce something a supplier can open without a login. The couples who are already organised will find it more useful, not less — because it holds more without more effort. The couples who are not organised will not touch the spreadsheet, which is already the case. This does not compete with a spreadsheet. It replaces the things a spreadsheet was never good at.

**"We don't want to be responsible for software."**

You are not. The venue has no admin panel, no user accounts to manage, no support queue. If a couple has a problem with the product, they contact Signal Studio. The venue's exposure is handing a code and optionally passing the note. If a code does not work, Signal Studio fixes it. This is closer to recommending a restaurant than running one.

**"What if couples don't use it?"**

Some will not. That is fine. The couples who do use it will have a materially calmer planning experience, and that experience will carry your venue's name. The ones who do not redeem the code have lost nothing — they still have what they had. Usage is not the success metric on the venue's side. The venue's measure is whether the couples who do use it have fewer noisy touchpoints with the coordinator and a better experience overall. One out of five couples using it and having a notably cleaner planning year is still a return.

**"Why prepay?"**

Because the product is not subscription-first — it is relationship-first. Prepaying is the signal that tells Signal Studio this venue is serious and that Signal Studio should treat them as a founding partner, not a trial account. It also means the venue's team is never chased for a monthly payment and the couple is never asked to enter a card. The annual prepay is the mechanism that makes the couple's experience seamless — not from the couple's perspective (they never see a price) but from the venue's. One payment, one year, nothing to manage.

**"Is this just for weddings?"**

Not in the product — the suite is built for coordination across event types. But the Venue Edition as offered is for wedding venues specifically because that is the context where the pain is most legible and the couple-coordinator relationship is most defined. If a venue also runs corporate events, christenings, or other private bookings, the product works for those couples too. The codes are not restricted to wedding couples. The venue decides who gets one.

**"What happens after 18 months?"**

The couple's workspace drops to the free plan. They get a quiet prompt beforehand — not a countdown in their face, just a clear note that the venue's sponsorship is ending and they can continue for free or move to a paid plan if they want to keep using the full suite. The venue's co-branded eyebrow stays visible for the full 18 months, then it drops with the sponsorship. The couple's data does not disappear. Their notes, tasks, and timeline stay intact on the free tier. Nothing is held hostage.

**"What about our couples' data and privacy?"**

Signal Studio does not sell couple data, does not profile it for advertising, and does not share it with the venue. The venue cannot see inside a couple's workspace. The eyebrow is cosmetic — it does not give the venue a dashboard into the couple's planning. The couple's data is theirs. The privacy policy is plain English. If a venue's legal team wants to review it, that is reasonable, and the answer will be short.

**"We're not technical."**

Neither are the couples using it. The product is deliberately plain. There is no onboarding sequence, no tutorial, no settings panel to configure. A couple redeems a code, lands in a workspace with their venue's name at the top, and starts writing. The coordinator who gets the walkthrough is not expected to become a power user — they just need to know what it looks like from the couple's side, so they can answer if a couple asks. That takes 30 minutes, once.

## Qualification criteria

### Who is a fit

The ideal founding venue:

- **Premium.** Not expensive-per-night as the only signal — premium in the sense that the brand promise is care, not throughput. A venue that sells being looked after.
- **Owner-operator who can sign alone.** No procurement committee, no regional manager who needs approval from a parent group. One person who feels the pain and can write the cheque. This is non-negotiable for the pilot stage — the buyer and the decision-maker must be the same person, or the sale cannot close in a reasonable timeframe.
- **Real coordination volume.** Roughly ≥ 40 weddings per year. Below that, the coordination pain is real but diffuse — the venue may not feel it sharply enough to pay to fix it. Above 40, the admin compounds visibly each season.
- **Feels repetitive admin pain.** The owner or head coordinator can name specific recurring questions without prompting. If they say "it's actually fine" or "we have a system," the pain may not be live. It may also mean the system is working, in which case this is not the right moment.
- **Sells a looked-after feeling.** The product protects a brand promise. If the brand promise is throughput, the venue will not spend €1,500 to reduce emails. If the brand promise is care, €1,500 to reduce noise while keeping their name on the experience is a straightforward decision.

### Disqualifiers

- **Budget venue.** The incentive is missing. A venue that competes on price does not have a brand promise that coordination confusion erodes. Do not chase down-market to fill a pilot.
- **No single decision-maker.** If the person who feels the pain cannot sign the invoice — if they need to go to a hotel group, a committee, or a chain's procurement team — the sale will stall in the gap between the sufferer and the buyer. The pilot is too short and the team too small to navigate that. Warm note and revisit in 12 months when there is a case study.
- **Buyer and sufferer are different people with no path between them.** A GM who feels no admin pain and a coordinator who cannot sign is a dead end. If the GM is genuinely interested and can get to the coordinator who feels it, there may be a path — but only if the GM can also sign. If neither can, move on.
- **Coordinator-only contact with no owner access.** Coordinators can be champions. They are rarely buyers. If the outreach has only reached a coordinator and there is no visible path to the owner, do not invest in the relationship as if it will close. It may open the door; it will not close the deal.

## Success metrics

These map directly to the five in the spine (§6). For a single pilot venue specifically, here is what "good" looks like.

| Metric (spine §6) | For a single pilot venue | "Good" threshold |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Qualified reply | Owner or decision-maker responds, engages on substance, does not say no | One genuine response from the right person |
| 2. Booked call | A scheduled conversation — not a "send me more info" handoff | One call, owner present |
| 3. Paid pilot | Prepaid invoice settled before codes are issued | One invoice paid. This is the proof gate. |
| 4. Couples activated | Couples who opened a workspace and used it beyond day one (i.e., added a note or a task) | ≥ 30% of codes redeemed; ≥ 50% of redeemed workspaces used past day one |
| 5. Shared artifacts | A Timeline forwarded outside the workspace — to a supplier, family member, or planner | ≥ 1 per active workspace over the 18-month window |

A pilot venue that settles the invoice, sees 30% of codes redeemed, and produces at least three shared Timelines is a strong result. That is enough to write a specific case study and to justify the outreach of a second wave.

A pilot venue that settles the invoice but sees zero couples activate is a stop signal — not a reason to iterate the venue pitch. That means the product is the problem. Do not send the second wave until the activation gap is understood.

## Kill triggers

The spine (§8) pre-committed the kills. Here they are operationalised for the venue motion specifically.

### Kill the venue wedge

**Trigger:** 20 founder-signed outreaches sent. Calls produced from those outreaches complete. Zero venues sign a paid pilot within 60 days of the first outreach batch going out. The stated reason across at least three conversations is a variation of "not worth paying for" — not timing, not "we're mid-season," not "come back in Q1."

**What it means:** the market is saying no to the thesis, not to the execution. The assumption that a premium venue will pay to reduce coordination chaos for its couples was wrong, or the price point is wrong, or the product is not yet credible enough to pay for. The next move is not a rebrand or a redesign of the page — it is a genuine pause to decide whether the thesis survives adjustment or not.

**What it does not mean:** a single venue saying "not worth it" is not the trigger. Bad timing is not the trigger. "We already have something" is not the trigger unless it recurs and the thing they have is genuinely solving the problem. The trigger is the pattern, not one data point.

**Pre-committed date:** 60 days from the date the first outreach batch is sent. This date should be recorded in the HQ decisions file the day outreach begins.

### Pivot the buyer

**Trigger:** venues engage warmly, show genuine interest, ask good questions — but cannot sign because the decision belongs to someone else. The person the outreach reaches is the sufferer, not the buyer. This pattern appears across ≥ 3 calls within 30 days.

**Move:** shift the wedge to wedding planners or independent coordinators who own their own P&L. They feel the pain, they can sign, and they serve the same cohort of couples. The product does not change. The positioning shifts from "stand behind your couples" to "run a cleaner, calmer practice." This is a pivot, not a kill.

### Stop — do not pivot

**Trigger:** venues sign paid pilots but couples do not activate (metric 4 flat — below 10% of codes redeemed, or redeemed workspaces idle after day one). This pattern confirmed in the review of the first paid pilot.

**Move:** do not send the second outreach wave. Do not refine the venue pitch. Fix the product — the onboarding, the first-run experience, the code redemption flow, whatever the review identifies. The wedge is not the problem. The product is. Spending more outreach into a broken activation loop is a way to burn the founding list.

**Why this is "stop" and not "pivot":** pivoting implies the direction is wrong. If couples are not activating, the direction is right — the couple is the end user, the venue is the buyer, that is the model. But the product is not yet delivering the calm it promises at first touch. That is a product fix, not a strategic pivot. Come back to outreach once the fix is confirmed in a second cohort.
