## The one-line story

Planning confusion creates hidden venue admin. Signal Studio creates calm — Notes where it starts, Tasks where it happens, Timeline what everyone sees, Signal what needs attention — and it carries the venue's name.

---

## Tone and anti-brief

**What this should feel like:** the product itself. Quiet. Specific. Nothing you have to interpret. A venue owner watching it should feel a small recognition — "that is what happens" — before they feel anything else.

**What it must not feel like:**

- A SaaS explainer (no feature callouts, no UI animations that say "look, it has this"). Product surfaces appear as context, not as demos.
- A hype reel (no tempo music, no quick cuts to prove energy, no text flying in from screen edges).
- A wedding-influencer aesthetic (no floral overlays, no golden-hour B-roll, no couples running on beaches). The subject is planning, not romance.
- A voice-over that closes with a call to action. No "book your demo today." No "join hundreds of venues." None of it.

The register is calm, premium, founder-led. If the editor's instinct is to add something, subtract it instead and see whether the cut is stronger.

### Framing notes

- All product surfaces: use real data. A couple named "Sarah & Tom" or "James & Clara" — common Irish names, specific enough to feel real. Planning a 2027 wedding. Real tasks, real timeline items (venue deposit, florist briefing, ceremony music, menu tasting). Not "Lorem Ipsum Couple."
- Coordinator desk: natural, not staged. A real desk or a desk that could be real. The inbox visible but not dramatised — do not emphasise the volume of email, just let it be present.
- The venue eyebrow on-screen: it should be readable. Frame so the venue name is legible without zooming in. This is the single most important product shot in the piece.
- Signal: the three lines should be real Signal Studio prose — not placeholder copy. Pull from the shipped briefing engine. They should read like plain English observations, not system outputs.

### No-VO variant

All three cuts work without voiceover. The structure holds on text cards and product surfaces alone. This variant is preferable for:

- Website autoplay (muted by default in browsers).
- LinkedIn without captions in the first three seconds.
- Any context where VO audio cannot be guaranteed.

In the no-VO variant: the opening text card stays. All lower-thirds stay. The coordinator desk shot needs a text card overlay to carry the "confusion is structural" moment (in the VO version, that is a text card anyway — it survives). The closing card stays. The only beats that lose explanatory weight without VO are the product surface transitions — keep the lower-thirds visible for longer (hold each text card two seconds beyond what the VO version would).

## Product shots

Exact surfaces to capture. In order of appearance.

### 1. Notes — new entry being written

- Surface: Notes product, capture view, a couple writing their first note.
- Content: a real note — something like "talked to the florist today — she can do the arch but not the table runners. need to decide whether to hire someone else or simplify the table arrangement. Tom prefers simpler." Specific, personal, real.
- Framing: phone in hand or laptop on a kitchen table. The venue eyebrow visible at the top of the screen — legible, not cropped. The note text legible enough to read.
- What it must show: the eyebrow. That is the whole venue brand point.

### 2. Tasks — one item completing

- Surface: Tasks product, list or board view, a real wedding task list.
- Content: real task names — "Confirm florist arrangement", "Send guest list to caterer", "Schedule menu tasting", "Pay venue deposit second instalment". One task being checked off (preferably "Pay venue deposit second instalment" — shows real money, real stakes).
- Framing: close enough to read the task names. Not so close that only one task is visible.
- What it must show: the plain-language register of the tasks — no project-manager jargon. The checked item should produce a quiet, satisfying visual state.

### 3. Timeline — public viewer

- Surface: Timeline product, public view. This is the view a supplier opens without an account.
- Content: a real wedding planning timeline. Milestones: "Venue booked", "Guest list first draft", "Save the dates sent", "Florist confirmed", "Menu tasting", "Ceremony music locked", "Final headcount". Plain English. Dates visible. No placeholder text.
- Framing: wide — this should read like a web page, not a phone screen. The couple's name visible at the top. The venue name visible if it can be shown on the Timeline surface.
- What it must show: that this is legible to someone who did not build it. It should look like something you could forward to your mother or your caterer.

### 4. Signal — morning briefing

- Surface: Signal product, morning briefing view.
- Content: three real briefing items, pulled from the shipped prose engine. Something like: "You have a menu tasting in four days and the catering confirmation is still pending." / "The guest list has moved three times this week — the florist count may be out of date." / "Two tasks in the ceremony section have been sitting since last Tuesday." Plain English. Specific. Not generic.
- Framing: phone or desktop — either works. Close enough to read the three lines. Nothing else on screen.
- What it must show: that this is three things, not thirty. Sparse by design.

### 5. The venue eyebrow — tight crop

- Surface: Notes or Tasks product, top of screen.
- Content: the venue name in the eyebrow position. Small. The product content below it. A specific venue name — something like "The Barnsgrove" or "Kilruddery" or whichever venue is the pilot — if it can be a real founding venue name with permission, do it. If not, use a plausible Irish venue name.
- Framing: close crop on the top of the screen. The eyebrow should be in the upper third of the frame. The product content visible but secondary.
- What it must show: that this is quiet. An eyebrow, not a logo wall. If it looks like advertising, the framing is wrong.

## Motion direction

**Pacing:** slow and deliberate. Each shot holds longer than instinct says. A shot that feels finished is not finished — hold it two seconds more. The edit should breathe.

**Transitions:** cuts only. No dissolves, no fades mid-sequence except on the opening and closing cards (which fade in and out). No swipe transitions, no motion-graphic wipes.

**Easing register:** if any motion is used (text cards fading in, lower-thirds appearing), use the product's own gesture vocabulary: slow ease-in, longer hold, slow ease-out. Not a snap. Not a bounce. The easing should feel like something settling, not something arriving. Reference the suite's CSS easing values: `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` — standard, calm, no personality flourish.

**Text card animation:** fade in only. No vertical slide, no kinetic-type animation, no letter-by-letter reveal. The text appears. It holds. It fades out if the next shot requires it. Simple.

**Product surface animation:** if product interaction is shown (typing a note, checking a task), it should feel at human speed — not accelerated, not slowed down. The interaction is not the subject. The surface is.

**Do not use:** kinetic typography. Parallax. Motion blur. Speed ramps. Split screens. Logo animations. Zoom-punch cuts. Any cut that communicates energy through pace. If the edit is exciting, it is wrong.

## Sound direction

**Ambient audio:** quiet room presence throughout. The coordinator desk scene: faint keyboard sound, a door in the distance if the venue allows. Not designed ambient — recorded ambient. The difference is audible.

**No music track:** this is not the piece for a soundtrack. Music under a video about calm would be the wrong signal. The absence of music is itself the statement.

**Where silence is deliberate:**

- The opening card (90s): complete silence. The viewer adjusts to the quiet before anything else happens.
- The "silence is the signal" card: the on-screen text is the moment. Any sound under it would contradict it. Silence.
- The closing card: hold the silence for the full duration of the card. Do not fade in a subtle tone at the end. The wordmark appears into silence. That is the close.

**Sound principle:** "silence is the signal" is a product line and a production value. Every moment where audio is absent should feel chosen, not empty. If the editor's instinct is to fill a silent moment with music, ask whether the silence is doing something. It usually is.

**VO recording:** one take that sounds like a conversation, not a performance. If the VO sounds like a radio presenter, re-record. If it sounds like someone explaining something to a person they know, it is right.

## Variants

### Website — signalstudio.ie/venues

- **Cut:** 30s.
- **Autoplay:** yes, muted. Loop-safe — the closing card must cut cleanly back to the opening card without a visible jump. Hold the last frame of the closing card for two seconds before the loop restarts, or build a two-second black/white fade-to-start.
- **Caption:** not required for muted autoplay on site, but a transcript should be available on the page for accessibility.
- **Position on page:** in the hero or just below it — not buried. This is the shop window for a venue owner who arrived on the page.
- **Aspect ratio:** 16:9 for desktop. On mobile, the loop-safe cut either crops to a vertical safe zone or is hidden in favour of a static frame.

### LinkedIn — founder-posted

- **Cut:** 30s (founders post the 30s; 60s is too long for the native scroll context).
- **Captioned:** yes. Full caption track matched to every on-screen text card and VO line. Assume no audio is playing.
- **First three seconds (survives no-audio):** the opening text card — "Planning a wedding is one of the most complex personal projects most people take on." — or, if the 30s begins in the venue space, a lower-third that appears immediately: "The noise in a planning year reaches your team as admin." The first three seconds cannot be a wide shot with no text. Someone scrolling without audio needs a reason to stay.
- **Caption for the post itself (not the video):** short, founder-voiced, no exclamation. Something like: "We built this for the venues that already care about the planning experience. A quiet version of what it is." Not a caption that explains the video — a caption that earns the play.

### Outreach — 1:1 email embed

- **Cut:** 30s. The 30s is the most personal cut — short enough to watch in the middle of a working day, long enough to land the point.
- **Format:** a thumbnail with a play link to the YouTube-hosted video. Not an attached file. Not an embedded autoplay. A thumbnail that shows the venue promise card, a play button, and a clean link.
- **Framing in the email:** one sentence before the link. Nothing that oversells it. Something like: "I put together a short piece — 30 seconds — that shows what this looks like for a couple. Worth a watch if you have a moment." Then the link. Then continue with the email. Do not make the video the subject of the email — make it an aside.
- **Subject:** the video should not appear in the subject line. The outreach email earns attention on its own terms; the video is supporting evidence, not the hook.
