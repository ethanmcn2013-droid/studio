# The Venue Setup Ritual

Status: production-ready · 2026-05-16
Owner: founder · use this verbatim in every pilot venue conversation
Source: competitor review (Superhuman-1, 3-director unanimous). Spine: `BUSINESS_PARTNER_REVIEW_2026_05.md` §4–§5.

This is the founder-led setup, run once per pilot venue, ~25 minutes. It is not a demo and not a sales call. It ends with one real couple's workspace standing under the venue's name — a forwardable Timeline the venue can see. The venue watches the proof get built; it does not hear a pitch.

The rule under everything: **the venue does nothing. You build. They watch their name go on calm work.** That is the patronage thesis made physical.

---

## Before the call (15 min prep, the day before)

1. Pick one real, near-term wedding the venue actually has on the books. Ask for it in the booking email: *"Bring one wedding you've got coming up — a real one, with real dates. We'll set it up live."* A real one, never a sample. The demo-vs-reality gap is a named risk; do not introduce it here.
2. Have the venue's name ready exactly as they'd want it read — the eyebrow line, not a logo.
3. Open three things only: a blank workspace, the example Timeline (`docs/strategy/VENUE_EXAMPLE_ROADMAP.md`) for your own reference on shape, and nothing else. No slides. No deck. No feature list.
4. Know the one sentence you are proving: *fewer confused couples, fewer repetitive emails, a better planning experience under your name.* Everything in the 25 minutes serves that sentence or it is cut.

---

## The 25 minutes

### Part 1 — Listen (10–12 min). You build nothing yet.

Open with one line: *"Walk me through a wedding from your side. Where does it get noisy?"*

Then stay quiet. You are listening for three things and writing them down:

- **The repeated question.** The thing couples email about over and over ("what time can we get in", "when do you need final numbers", "is the menu locked"). This becomes the spine of the Timeline.
- **The handoff that drops.** Where the couple, the planner, and the venue's own team fall out of sync. This is where the forwardable artifact earns its place.
- **The name moment.** How the venue wants to be seen by the couple during planning. Quiet pride, not branding. This sets the eyebrow tone.

Do not pitch in this part. If they ask "so what does it do" — *"I'll show you in a minute, with your wedding, not a sample."* Then back to listening.

### Part 2 — Build (12–15 min). They watch their wedding become calm.

Now build the real workspace, narrating only what you do, in plain English, never naming a feature:

1. **Capture, in their words.** Take the repeated question from Part 1 and put the first three things into the plan as the couple would say them — "Confirm final numbers", "Lock the menu", "Agree arrival time". No jargon enters. They watch their own language become the plan.
2. **The forwardable view.** Open the public Timeline link. Show them what the couple sees with no account, no login, nothing to learn — the plan, in plain English, with the venue's name in a quiet line at the top. Say one sentence: *"This is the email you stop having to send."*
3. **The name, quietly.** Put the venue's name on it the way they asked in Part 1. One line. Not a logo wall. Let them see it sit there calmly.
4. **The handoff.** Show that the same link works for the couple, the planner, and their own coordinator — one artifact, no one logging in. This is the dropped-handoff from Part 1, closed, in front of them.

Stop building at 15 minutes even if it is not "finished". A standing real workspace beats a polished imaginary one.

### The close (2 min). No ask for the sale. One ask only.

*"That's the whole thing on your side. You hand a couple a code, they get this, your name's on it, nothing for your team to run. Venue Edition is €1,500 a year, prepaid. If you're one of the first fifteen, that price is locked for as long as you stay. I don't need an answer now. I need to know: is this worth a real conversation with whoever signs?"*

Then stop talking. The proof was the 25 minutes, not the price.

---

## After the call (10 min, same day)

- Send one short note. Not a recap deck. One paragraph: the link to the workspace you built (it persists), the year, the founding lock, one line. The artifact does the follow-up.
- Log only against the five metrics (`BUSINESS_PARTNER_REVIEW_2026_05.md` §6): did this become a qualified reply, a booked call with the signer, a paid pilot. Nothing else gets a number.
- If they went cold in Part 1 ("we already use a planner / a spreadsheet") — that is the buyer≠sufferer signal. Note it against the qualification criteria; do not chase.

---

## What this ritual refuses (hold these — they are the moat in the room)

- **No slides, no deck, no recorded demo.** The build is the demo. A sample wedding is the demo-vs-reality risk; refuse it.
- **No feature names.** You never say a product name to the venue. You say "the plan", "the link", "your name on it". The conversational register drops "Signal" by design.
- **No scaled team.** This is founder-run, ≤20 venues, by design. It is a proof instrument, not an onboarding programme. Do not promise it scales — it is not meant to yet.
- **No "automatically".** Nothing in the narration implies the software decides, predicts, or moves their work. A human builds it while they watch. That is the trust asset, stated by doing.
- **No price-led open.** The price comes last, after the proof, once, then silence.

If the ritual ever needs a slide to land, the product is not ready — fix the product, not the ritual.
